# Rate-Setting Worksheet

*Most freelancers set rates by guessing what feels "not too greedy." This worksheet replaces the guess with arithmetic. Work through it with real numbers — it takes about 20 minutes.*

## Step 1 — Your Real Cost of Living (annual)

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Personal living costs (rent, food, transport, family) | $______ |
| Health insurance & medical | $______ |
| Retirement savings (aim for 10–15% of target income) | $______ |
| Taxes (self-employed: estimate 25–35% of gross — you pay both halves now) | $______ |
| Business costs (software, hardware, workspace, accounting) | $______ |
| Buffer for slow months (aim: 10% of the total above) | $______ |
| **A. Total you must earn** | **$______** |

## Step 2 — Your Real Billable Hours (annual)

Start with 2,080 (40 hrs × 52 weeks), then subtract honestly:

| Deduction | Typical | Yours |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| Vacation + holidays + sick time | −240 hrs | −____ |
| Marketing, sales calls, proposals | −300 hrs | −____ |
| Admin, invoicing, email | −200 hrs | −____ |
| Learning & skill development | −100 hrs | −____ |
| Unbillable gaps between projects | −200 hrs | −____ |
| **B. Realistic billable hours** | **≈1,040** | ______ |

**The takeaway most people miss:** you can bill roughly *half* of your working hours. This is why "my old salary ÷ 2,080" produces a rate that slowly bankrupts you.

## Step 3 — Your Floor Rate

> **A ÷ B = your minimum hourly rate: $______/hr**

This is your **floor** — the rate below which working is losing money. It is not your price. Your price is the floor plus profit and market positioning:

| Multiplier | When |
|-----------|------|
| Floor × 1.3 | You're newer, building a portfolio deliberately |
| Floor × 1.5–2 | You have results and testimonials (most freelancers belong here) |
| Floor × 2–3+ | You specialize in a niche with measurable business outcomes |

**My target rate: $______/hr**

## Step 4 — Convert to Project Pricing (recommended)

Hourly pricing punishes you for being fast. For defined projects, quote fixed prices built from your target rate:

1. Estimate hours honestly: ______ hrs
2. Add 20–30% (revisions, communication, the unexpected — this is not padding, it's realism): ______ hrs
3. × target rate = **base project price: $______**
4. Gut-check against the client's value: if the project makes/saves the client 10× your price, you're in healthy territory — and there's room for your Option B (see Proposal Template).

## Step 5 — The Raise Schedule

Rates are not a one-time decision. Put these triggers in your calendar right now:

- [ ] **Every 2 new clients:** quote the next prospect 10–15% higher. New clients have no anchor — they simply meet the new you.
- [ ] **At 80% capacity for 2+ months:** raise rates across the board. Fully booked at your current rate *is* the market telling you you're underpriced.
- [ ] **Annually, existing clients:** +5–10% with 6–8 weeks' notice (Email Script #9 handles this conversation).
- [ ] **When you'd dread the project:** quote the "I'd be happy at this number" price. The worst case is a no; the best case is being paid properly for hard work.

## Quick Reality Checks

- If **every** prospect says yes instantly, your rate is too low. A healthy close rate on proposals is 40–60% — below that, fix your positioning; above ~80%, raise prices.
- Never justify your rate with your costs ("I have to charge this because insurance..."). Clients pay for outcomes. Justify with results.
- The discomfort of quoting a higher number lasts ten seconds. The cost of a too-low rate lasts the entire project.
